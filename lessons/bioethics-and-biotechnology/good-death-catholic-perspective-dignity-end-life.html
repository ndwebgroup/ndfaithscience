The Good Death: A Catholic Perspective on Dignity at the End of Life

  • Edit
  • Delete
This unit explores end of life issues with an emphasis on understanding how we are called to support those who are dying and how we especially have a duty to protect, pray, and advocate for those who are vulnerable to the evils of capital punishment, suicide, and euthanasia. As students analyze Church documents, learn about the history of end of life legislation, investigate connections of science and medicine, and reflect on their own prior knowledge and attitudes about end of life issues, they will come to integrate this knowledge and synthesize a fuller understanding of the complexities of the passage of the human being from life into death and ultimately Resurrection in Jesus Christ.

This unit was designed for a three week time frame in a senior honors level bioethics course. Each lesson is designed to be completed in a 45 minute class period, but can easily be adapted to block schedules by allowing students more time for reading, processing, and reflection.

Essential Questions

1. What does it mean to live and die with dignity?

2. Who should have the final say on ending life?

3. As Catholics, what do we do when end of life decisions conflict between civil law and moral law?

4. Empathetic action or allowable suffering in a time of death: what would Christ say?

 

Knowledge

  • Students will comprehend the dignity of the human person and outline how this dignity entitles each person to “a good death” that includes compassionate end of life care.
  • Students will understand the Church’s position on capital punishment and apply these principles to justifying the ethics of executing prisoners.
  • Students will explain the science of the effects of lethal injection on the body, terminal and progressive illness, reduced consciousness (coma, persistent vegetative state, and brain death), and the changes the body goes through in the process of dying.
  • Students will describe Church teaching on suicide and euthanasia, particularly in morally complex situations that involve progressive disease or reduced consciousness of the patient.
  • Students will recall the Church’s attentive nature to the dying person throughout history and at present citing examples such as the Corporal Work of Mercy to Bury the Dead, the Sacrament of Anointing the Sick, and the long tradition of health care for the sick and dying.

Skills

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

  • navigate and analyze Church documents, commentary, and websites on end of life care
  • communicate to their family and peers the teachings of the Church on the moral issues of capital punishment, euthanasia, and end of life care
  • apply their knowledge of ordinary and extraordinary means as well as their understanding of the physiology of terminal illness and reduced consciousness (coma, persistent vegetative state, and brain death) to evaluate the ethics of withdrawing life-support in various case studies
  • apply Catholic social teaching in the context of the racial inequalities of capital punishment to advocate for marginalized populations
  • form their consciences about end of life issues

Method

Day 1: Catholicism and the “Good Death”

Introductory rhetorical questions:  Ask students, "What constitutes a 'good death'? Is it peaceful? Surrounded with family? Where does it take place?" On the contrary, what is a “bad death”? Is it violent, vengeful, murderous, lonely? What about a “bad death” specifically makes it bad?

Explain to students that we will be examining the features of death in bad and good circumstances and what our faith teaches about death

Demonstration: Show students a $20 and ask its worth. Invite students up to the front to harm the bill in some way (step, draw, etc.) Then ask students if they would still want to keep the bill in its current condition. Explain that the value of the bill doesn’t decrease based on its appearance. Similarly, human life always has an inherent worth and dignity.

Explore: What is dignity? Ask the students if they can begin to construct a working definition together. Write some thoughts on the side of the whiteboard and revisit this throughout the unit as new insights arise; we will revise our understanding of this word throughout the unit.

Read-Aloud: John Paul II: Dying with Dignity

HW: Consider the following questions from today’s reading and jot down some thoughts to share with the class tomorrow.

 

Day 2: The Dignity of the Human Person

At the beginning of class, give each student a green colored pen to add to their notes from yesterday. As the discussion progresses and after the discussion, students will have 10 minutes to record any new insights they have from the reading. This reflection sheet will be collected at the end of class as a formative assessment.

Socratic Seminar Style Discussion: Pope John Paul refers to the words from Romans, 14: 7-8 as he is clear about life and death in this world. “ None of us lives to himself , and none of us dies to himself. If we live, we live to the Lord, and if we die, we die to the Lord; so then, whether we live or whether we die, we are the Lord’s “

How does the word “ dignity “ arise from these words Pope John Paul uses in his message ?

The Pope ends his presentation by saying “Last but not least, respect for the dignity and sanctity of life of patients includes concern for their spiritual needs.“ Reflecting on the suffering that might occur in an end of life issue, what are your thoughts on the physical and spiritual needs of the dying ?

Debrief: Students will have 10 minutes to write down any new insights they have after the group discussion and they will hand in their sheet at the end of class.

HW: Survey on Google Forms: Examination of Current Attitudes Toward Death (Teacher Version, Student Version)

 

Day 3: Attitudes Toward Death and Introduction to Capital Punishment

Analysis of Yesterday’s Survey Results: Project the results of the anonymous survey on the board. Ask students: were the majority of students in favor of euthanasia or against it? What about capital punishment? Discuss the results in more depth and ask students if they were surprised at the results.

Lecture: History and Science of Capital Punishment in the US

HW: Watch: TedX Talk “Medical Assistance in Dying: It’s not as easy as it looks” with Dr. Joel Zivot (8:36-14:10 only).

Compose a paragraph reflection on the following prompts:
Currently, the US uses lethal injection for the death penalty becaue it is viewed as the most humane way to die. Based on Dr. Zivot’s analysis of post lethal injection autopsies, is this claim supported or unsupported? Justify your answer by citing specific physiological evidence.

“There are cases in which executions fail. Albeit they are few and far between, but what do we do in these cases? Is there a moral change in one’s thinking when death does not come quickly?”

 

Day 4: Capital Punishment Research

Introductory Video to pique interest from Dead Man Walking that shows an emotional moment between death row inmate Matthew Poncelet with Sr. Helen Prejean.

*If your schedule permits, you may consider watching the entire movie with your class over a few days*

Discuss: What is the essential Church teaching being illustrated here? What have you heard the Church teach about capital punishment? Within Catholicism there are some nuanced teachings on when capital punishment is permissible, and doctrine has unfolded as security of prisons has modernized.

Research: Divide students into two large teams today to research the arguments for and against capital punishment citing evidence from Church documents as well as any other ethical arguments or case studies they feel are relevant. Students will be aware of which side of the argument they will be debating tomorrow in order that they may focus their research on one side in depth. To help organize their thoughts before and during the debate, they should make debate notes using the following template.

In addition to the following resources, which should be printed and distributed, students are strongly encouraged to find more documents on this topic on the USCCB website using their Chromebooks. If your school does not have 1:1 devices like Chromebooks or iPads, you may chose to coordinate this as a day in the on-campus library.

Abolish the Death Penalty - Amnesty International

Catholic Answers 

Pope Francis changes teaching on the death penalty 

Catholic Defense of Capital Punishment

Bishop’s Statement on Capital Punishment (1980)

A Culture of Life and the Penalty of Death (2005)

Recommended Case Studies: Timothy McVeigh, Walter Barton, Johnny D (“Walter McMillan” from the movie Just Mercy)

HW: Complete the debate template.

 

Day 5: Capital Punishment Debate

Today students will be debating the arguments for and against capital punishment. They may reference their debate template notes from yesterday.

HW: Complete a one page reflection on the following questions, taking into consideration the points that were brought up in today’s debate. You will be turning in these questions as well as your debate note sheet on Monday.

  1. What are your thoughts on capital punishment after today’s debate? Are you more for the death penalty or against it? Answer honestly, but use information from the debate today to support your points.
  2. Are some victims of capital punishment more deserving than others?
  3. Does God consider perpetrators of capital crimes less worthy of forgiveness than others?
  4. Is the death penalty a symptom of a culture of violence or a solution to it?

 

Day 6: Capital Punishment, Mass Incarceration, and Systemic Racism

Introduction: Ask students, "What were some of the most compelling arguments FOR the death penalty from yesterday’s discussion? What were some of the most compelling arguments AGAINST it? Do you think all people on death row deserve to be there? Why or why not?" Explain that today we will be considering why there is injustice in our legal system, and tomorrow we will dive deeper into what forensic science can do to minimize wrongful convictions to prevent innocent people from being put on death row.

Video: to spark discussion of today’s lesson key question. Students will then consider the following question in a Think-Pair-Share framework:

Why are the incarceration rates disproportionately high for people of color? Depending on student insights, teachers may want students to lead this discussion or may want to introduce some more in depth studies and reasons on this phenomenon. A good starting place can be found here.

Interview with Bryan Stevenson. After watching, discuss the following points: How does our identity as a country shape our beliefs about the death penalty? Is inequality an issue that affects everyone or just on those in prison? Remind students that this is the real lawyer behind the movie Just Mercy, which is available for free as of June 2020.

HW: Students will complete a journal entry to reflect and process any of the topics over the last few days they wish. They may reflect on what they learned about the intertwining of social justice, mass incarceration and capital punishment.

 

Day 7: Capital Punishment, Mass Incarceration, and Systemic Racism Continued

Innocence Project Webquest: Students will examine the Innocence Project website and work in pairs to complete a guided worksheet that explains the causes of wrongful convictions and what can be done to prevent them.

HW: Students will be assigned one of four resources to investigate to help prepare for tomorrow’s jigsaw discussion on suicide. If you are unfamiliar with how to conduct a jigsaw discussion, more in depth instructions can be found here. Additionally, each article should be posted as a separate item on Google Classroom or your school’s learning management system (LMS) to allow students working on the same article to share their reflections on their assigned reading tonight.

Articles/Video: 

  1. Catholic Answers - What does the Church Teach About Suicide?
  2. Every Suicide is Tragic
  3. Depression and Suicide: A Catholic Perspective
  4. Video: Talking About Suicide

 

Day 8: What does the Church Teach About Suicide?

Spectrum Statements: Open class by instructing students to stand in the middle of the room. Then, instruct students to walk to one side of the room (or somewhere slightly off the middle) based on their opinions of suicide in light of yesterday’s reading. You may also decide to put tape on the floor to make these gradients clearer. Read each statement one at a time and give students time to process, reflect, and share their reasoning for where they are standing. An example of this activity can be seen here, and the statements to read are below:

  • Suicide is always tragic
  • Suicide is never morally acceptable
  • Suicide should be illegal
  • Suicide precludes a soul from entering heaven

Jigsaw Discussion: As all students have already examined some of their personal beliefs in the first activity today and discussed their articles/video in expert groups the night before, spend the last half of class discussing the following questions in mixed groups. Students will then have the last 5-10 minutes to complete the worksheet with the insights they learned from the spectrum activity and jigsaw discussion before submitting it as a formative assessment.

HW: Students will compose a list of questions they have for the social worker about suicide in preparation for tomorrow's Q&A 

 

Day 9: Suicide

Guest Speaker: Invite the School Social Worker or Psychologist to speak about suicide. This piece can be tailored to fit the needs of each individual school, but important themes that would be useful are:

  • The romanticization of suicide in literature (Romeo and Juliet) and modern media (13 Reasons Why)
  • How to recognize the symptoms of depression and advocate for your peers and others who may be facing this
  • Coping strategies and anti bullying resources
  • Pastoral Care: how school faculty, staff, admin, and priests can guide students through mental health issues, connect them to the necessary resources, and care for the school community in times of crisis by fostering an open and caring dialogue about suicide

HW: 

  1. Read Ethical and Religious Directives for Catholic Health Care Services: Fifth Edition USCCB (read Part 5 only)
  2. Answer reading guide questions for Monday (Day 11). This will be counted as a quiz grade.

 

Day 10: Introduction to Euthanasia

Building a Glossary: To better understand and sift through the difficult topic of euthanasia, it is useful to develop a working vocabulary of terms, buzzwords, and people. Today students will be given editing access to a Google Sheet with terms they will frequently see in news stories about euthanasia. Each student will choose at least one term to define, cite a source for the definition, illustrate, and explain the significance of. (~20 minutes)

At the end of 20 minutes, the teacher will project the glossary on the board and address the definitions the students provided while sorting out misconceptions as they arise. If any definitions seem obviously incorrect, use it as a teachable moment to analyze the reliability of the source.  This Google Slides presentation can then be saved by the students and referenced as needed throughout the course of the next few days.

HW: 

1) Watch and read “How Brittany Maynard may change the right-to-die debate”

2) Read Seminarian with Brain Cancer to Brittany Maynard: Our Lives are Still Worth Living

3) Reflect: How are Brittany Maynard and Phillip G Johnson similar and different? Illustrate their view of death using your previous knowledge, the two texts, and as many glossary terms as possible.

 

Day 11: Science of Reduced Consciousness: Examining the Physiological Differences between Coma, PVS, and Brain Death

Intro: Hand out worksheet and gauge students’ prior knowledge about parts of the brain and their functions. Hand out colored pencils to color code various parts of the brain (5 min)

Video: Is a Brain Dead Person Actually Dead? 

Optional: To reteach/support content, refer students to Brain&Life article for supporting text (which also includes information about minimally conscious states)

Discuss: What are the differences between coma, persistent vegetative state, and brain death medically speaking? Which are defined as legally dead? If a person is legally dead is it morally permissible to remove life support that involves extraordinary means? What about ordinary means? 

Read-Aloud: Is there an official Roman Catholic teaching on brain death? –a response to  yesterday's claim from the National Catholic Bioethics Center (10 minutes)

Think-Pair Share: After reading the article, students will take 15 minutes to mentally compose their gleanings from the article, share with a partner, and then discuss in a small group.

**Enrichment Opportunity: Direct students who would like to investigate this further to the following article.

HW: Read a recent FAQ from the National Catholic Bioethics Center on Brain Death. In a paragraph or two, reflect on the new information. Does it change your understanding about brain death or death from today’s conversations?

 

Day 12 Case Study: Terri Schiavo

Case Study: Watch The Enduring Legacy of Terry Schiavo (New York Times video ~15 minutes)

Hand out video discussion questions. Allow students 5 minutes to compose their thoughts before getting into a roundtable discussion. 

Roundtable: Move student desks in a circle and go through the questions. Conclude the conversation by discussing these questions from author Trent Horn 

For students interested in learning more about similar cases, see the following resources (More from Dr. Owens, TED Talk by Martin Pistorius or the book authored by Pistorius  - Ghost Boy), 

HW: Tomorrow we will discuss the meaning of suffering in natural  death. To prepare for tomorrow’s discussion, watch “The Meaning of Suffering” by Fr. Mike Schmitz and read “Care of the Dying: A Catholic Perspective”. When you are finished, compose a 1 page reflection on what you watched and read.

 

Day 13: Natural Death with Dignity

Part 1: Lecture to recap yesterday’s reading

Part 2: Chapel Activity. The teacher will select and print 3-4 quotes from JPII’s Salvifici Doloris (particularly one on the redemptive value of the crucifix The teacher will then take the students to the chapel (make sure a crucifix is present)  to reflect on what they mean and how they relate their lives personally (~15 minutes)

Part 3: Make a plan to live out one Spiritual and one Corporal Work of Mercy as it applies to the sick and dying this week. Students can do this individually or as a class and are encouraged to come up with their own ideas. Some other ideas are listed below:

  • Visit a nursing home/hospice/elderly neighbor who is homebound
  • Pray a novena for someone they know who is sick
  • Donate lap blankets to a hospice or fleece “fidget blankets” to patients with Alzheimers

*Optional Resource for students to turnkey Catholic End of Life information to family members during conversations at home: Teachings of the Catholic Church: Caring for People at the End of Life

HW: After one week, students will write a 1-2 page reflection on 1) their experience in the Chapel Activity and which quote spoke most  and 2)  their experience completing the Spiritual and Corporal Works of Mercy of their choice.

 

Day 14: Bury the Dead: Examining Church teaching on burial and treatment of the body after death

Lecture*: What physiologically happens to a body as it dies? How have cultural practices, religion, and concerns about space shaped our burial customs throughout history? How does the Catholic understanding of the dignity of the human person tie in to how the body is treated when the soul is no longer present in it? Which methods of laying a person to rest are permitted or encouraged by the Church? Which ones are not?

*Note: Slide 3 contains information about the physiological changes a body goes through after death. It is not particularly graphic, but it is left up to the professional judgement of the teacher, their knowledge of their students, and the context of the class (for example, this information would be routine in Forensics, but perhaps atypical in traditional Apologetics) to decide if they want to show the first part of the Human Burial video on Slide 3 or jump directly to the second half on Slide 4*

Research Activity: Students will be provided with a printed set of sources on Catholic burial customs (see slides above) and work in teams of 4 or 5 to skim the sources and sill out the summary sheet on Treatment of the Body After Death.

Discussion: see slides above

HW: Work on template for End of Life Narrative

 

Day 15: Writing End of Life Narratives

Students will take out the template for the end of unit project they filled out yesterday and spend the class period continuing to construct a story in which a person faces one of the end of life issues covered in the unit (capital punishment, suicide, euthanasia, hospice, burial customs)

They will also compose an answer key on a separate document which explains if the conclusion of the story was morally acceptable or morally problematic, in light of Catholic teaching.

 

Day 16: Evaluating End of Life Narratives

Printed copies of end of life narratives and answer keys will be due today. Student authors will be evaluated on the detail of their narrative, as well as the reasoning and accuracy of the claims made in the answer key.

Additionally, today each student will receive another student’s story (without the answer key) and be charged with creating their own answer key  to justify whether or not the story demonstrated a conclusion that was morally acceptable or problematic. They may use any printed articles or  notes they wish, and they will submit their answer at the end of class as a summative assessment of the unit.

 

Assessment

Formative assessments are embedded in the daily lesson plans, a take home quiz is assigned on Day 9, and a summative unit assessment can be found on Days 15 and 16. Please refer to the Methods Section for assessments.

Standards

USCCB Standards

VI. Life in Jesus Christ, II. God Has Taught Us How to Live a New Life in Christ 

5) Fifth Commandment: You shall not kill. a) Respect human life in all its stages and situations (CCC, nos. 2258- 2262). b) Legitimate self-defense and the death penalty (CCC, nos. 2263-2267). c) Principles regarding health, science, bodily integrity (CCC, nos. 2292- 2301). d) Sins against the Fifth Commandment: murder; suicide; abortion; euthanasia; embryonic stem cell research; abuse of alcohol, drugs, food, or tobacco; abuse of the body (CCC, nos. 364, 2268-2283, 2290-2291).

 

V. Sacraments as Privileged Encounters with Jesus Christ, III. Sacraments of Healing 

B. Anointing of the Sick: the sacrament which gives spiritual healing and strength to a person seriously ill and sometimes also physical recovery (CCC, nos. 1499-1513). 1. Understanding the sacrament. a. Scriptural basis: Jas 5:14-15 (CCC, no. 1510). b. Historical development (CCC, no. 1512). c. Theology. 1) Illness (CCC, nos. 1500-1502). 2) Christ the Physician (CCC, no. 1503). 3) Faith and healing (CCC, no. 1504). 4) Christ’s suffering (CCC, no. 1505). 5) Disciples carry cross (CCC, no. 1506). 6) Holy Spirit’s gift of healing (CCC, no. 1509). 7) Christ institutes sacrament of the sick (CCC, nos. 1500-1513).

 

II. Who Is Jesus Christ?, V. Challenges, C. How can people say that God is good if suffering and evil are present in the world?

4. The Passion and Death of Jesus can help us to see beyond suffering and remind us that God is present with us in our suffering, pain, and death; our own personal suffering when united to Jesus’ suffering becomes redemptive for ourselves and others (CCC, no. 1851).

V. Sacraments as Privileged Encounters with Jesus Christ, V. Challenges to Worship and Sacraments 

3. The Sacrament of the Anointing of the Sick can have different effects. Sometimes Christ does bring about physical healing through that sacrament. Other times, the healing is spiritual in that it helps the person to be better prepared for death, to be at peace, and to be eager to be with the Lord.

 

NGSS Standards

LS1.A: Structure and Function

  • Systems of specialized cells within organisms help them perform the essential functions of life. (HS-LS1-1)

  • Multicellular organisms have a hierarchical structural organization, in which any one system is made up of numerous parts and is itself a component of the next level. (HS-LS1-2)

  • Feedback mechanisms maintain a living system’s internal conditions within certain limits and mediate behaviors, allowing it to remain alive and functional even as external conditions change within some range. Feedback mechanisms can encourage (through positive feedback) or discourage (negative feedback) what is going on inside the living system. (HS-LS1-3)
