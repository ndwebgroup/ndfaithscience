How do we Make Decisions That are Both Morally and Scientifically Informed?

  • Edit
  • Delete
This lesson plan investigates the relationship between science and religion by looking at the human response to a pandemic. The goal of the lesson and its exercises are to provide the tools for students to make decisions that are both morally and scientifically informed. (Mount Notre Dame High School)

Essential Questions

  1. How does science inform our moral judgments and the application of Catholic social teaching?
  2. What are examples of fundamental misunderstandings about science regarding the current global health crisis?  How do those misunderstandings lead to decisions that do not respect the dignity of all people? 
  3. What is meant by “common good” and how do we determine what the common good is?

Knowledge

The KNOWLEDGE that students participating in our lesson will gain are:

  1. Students will learn that Science and Religion are complementary which can help Catholics make informed and just decisions.
  2. Students will learn that the source of information can help determine the quality of the information and its usefulness.
  3. Students will learn how to make decisions that are both morally and scientifically informed.

 

Skills

  • NGSS skill:  obtaining, evaluating and communicating information through analysis of scenario data.
  • Ohio Learning Standards (OLS) (Page 3 - Vision; Page 3 - Goals): Formulate and revise explanations and models using logic and scientific evidence (critical thinking).

http://education.ohio.gov/getattachment/Topics/Learning-in-Ohio/Science/Ohios-Learning-Standards-and-MC/SciFinalStandardsMC060719.pdf.aspx?lang=en-US 

  • Nature of Science from the OLS (Page 3 - Vision): One goal of science education is to help students become scientifically literate citizens able to use science as a way of knowing about the natural and material world. All students should have sufficient understanding of scientific knowledge and scientific processes to enable them to distinguish what is science from what is not science and to make informed decisions about career choices, health maintenance, quality of life, community and other decisions that impact both themselves and others.

http://education.ohio.gov/getattachment/Topics/Learning-in-Ohio/Science/Ohios-Learning-Standards-and-MC/SciFinalStandardsMC060719.pdf.aspx?lang=en-US 

 

Method

The lesson will be completed in 1 ½ blocks or three periods.

Note that full blocks are 90 minutes and half blocks are 45 minutes. The school calendar alternates full and half blocks.


 

A. Formative Assessment (the “hook)  10 scenarios (could be homework the night before)

  • Students will be given a list of ten statements that describe situations that they are asked to agree or disagree with the statement, policy, or guideline, without looking up any additional information
  1. You should floss your teeth every day (American Dental Association) AGREE/DISAGREE
  2. You must be 13 years old to have an Instagram account. (Instagram Terms of Use) 

AGREE/DISAGREE

  1. A product’s BRAND is more important than the QUALITY of the product (a Tik Tok influencer) 

AGREE/DISAGREE

  1. During a pandemic, all physically able persons should wear a mask over the mouth and nose in public (CDC/WHO) 

AGREE/DISAGREE

  1. I can have a positive effect on climate change (www.nrdc.org)   

AGREE/DISAGREE

  1. Cell phones can cause brain cancer (News Media) 
  2. I will stop buying any product containing glitter because of microplastic concerns (Published research from Yale)

AGREE/DISAGREE

  1. Reducing the use of make-up is good for my health and the environment. (Alicia Keys)

AGREE/DISAGREE

  1. Animals should not be used for pharmaceutical testing. (PETA)

AGREE/DISAGREE

  1. “Dangerous” video trends on social media are actually pretty funny (Buzzfeed)

AGREE/DISAGREE

 (see Formative Assessment attachment)

 

Slide A: Topic Sentence, questions related to hook activity

 

TOPIC: How does morality and the nature of science intersect as they inform the decisions we make for the common good?

 

  1. Why did you pick the answer you did?
  2. Did the source of the information affect your decision?
  3. Did you factor in or consider others when making your decision?
  4. What would change your decision?  For example, would a strong opinion from your dentist convince you to change your decision (if you don’t floss every day)?



 

Slide B: Relationship of science and ethics - a fundamental misunderstanding

 

  • The Myth of the faith/science conflict
    • Faith and reason are like two wings on which the human spirit rises to the contemplation of truth; and God has placed in the human heart a desire to know the truth- in a word, to know himself- so that, by knowing and loving God, men and women may also come to the fullness of truth about themselves.” - Pope St. John Paul II
    • Catholic thinkers and theologians have always been deeply invested in and interested in science. It’s the beautiful complex creation that God has given us…we want to understand it!
    • Examples of Catholic scientists https://www.catholicscientists.org/catholic-scientists-of-the-past 

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Catholic_clergy_scientists 

 

Slide C

  • By looking around at creation, reason and experience can point to God’s existence (CCC, nos. 156-159).
  • The study of the world through the experience of doing science is an opportunity for us to be struck by the wonder at the complexity of it.

 

What do the two have to do with one another as it relates to decision making?

Slide D

  • Ethical decisions involve science, values, morals, as well as the consideration of the common good. We will look at some examples today.


 

  1. Nature of Science - Science approaches every investigation through the scientific method. This approach will yield information which can be used to formulate decisions.
  2. Values--what you feel is important when making a decision ex. cost, environmental impact, aesthetics
  3. Morals--how you use your values when making a decision
  4. Ethics--how you work through problems to determine the best course of action

Slide E

  1. The Common Good - From the Catechism, the common good is the “sum total of social conditions that allow people, either as groups or individuals, to reach their fulfillment more fully and more easily.”

The common good requires that individuals and societies

         1 – respect the basic, inalienable rights of each human being

         2 – promote the social well-being and development of \

various social groups

We must consider the effect of our decisions on OTHERS in our communities (both loved ones, acquaintances, AND strangers)

 

 

Slide F

  1. Catholic Social Teaching

To keep the common good in the forefront of our decision making, we must be familiar with the Catholic Social Teachings. Let’s review the 7 themes:

https://www.usccb.org/beliefs-and-teachings/what-we-believe/catholic-social-teaching/seven-themes-of-catholic-social-teaching

 

Slide G

  • Science can help inform moral principles or the application of moral principles by offering data that can be analyzed in conjunction with these principles
  • Science and religion can work together (science = how and religion = why) 

HOW: research, data

WHY: as a moral person, how you act with the information, motivations for your decision


 

Slide H

 Consider these questions when making an ethical/moral decisions:


 

  1. How do we find the known facts? How do we know those facts are true? What evidence would you look for to support the facts? 
  2. Who would you consult for more information? Professionals? Trusted counsel? Spiritual counsel?  Why?
  3. How do you assess people’s motives, intentions, and values? 
  4. What is the right personal code of ethics when considering the Common Good? 
  5. When we consider our faith, do we use religious tradition (ie. church documents, the Catechism, or scripture?
  6. We can also apply cardinal virtues to help guide us: Prudence (it is prudent to keep yourself informed), Wisdom (seek wisdom of those who know the situation well), Fortitude (have the courage to make right decisions), Temperance (sacrifice by individuals is often required for the common good).  Can you provide an example of your use of a Cardinal virtue over the last year?
  7. Once we decide on a course of action, how should we weigh the consequences of our decisions and their effect on others?
  8. How does your guide demonstrate the value of solidarity from Catholic Social Teaching? 





 

 

Slides I-L: Longer Scenarios

Fundamental misunderstandings about science and how decisions based on these misunderstandings can be contrary to the common good - This portion of the lesson will present  scenarios and decisions based on a misunderstanding of science. The conclusions will be shown to be contrary to Catholic Social Teaching. (½ block)

 

Scenario 1: Early on in the pandemic, the CDC recommended that hand washing several times daily is essential to preventing the spread of COVID-19.  Indoor congregations of groups indoors (such as for religious services, weddings, funerals, sporting events, in-person school) were also banned to avoid the spread of COVID-19.  

 

  1. For those that did comply with these bans, think of at least one inequality or injustice that occurred?  For that example, why was it unfair?
  2. For those that did not comply with these bans, was this in the interest of the common good?  Why or why not?

 

Scenario 2

 

 

  1. Set up an Aromatherapy diffuser.  Have students write the start time and then write down the time when they first smell the aroma on a shared document for the entire class to see.  They will also place a visual indicator (like a cup) on their desk once they smell the aroma.
  2. Once the teacher sees that all of the students have displayed the indicator(cup), the teacher can lead a discussion about the spread of aerosols.

http://web01.cabeard.k12.in.us/science/Bio2/bc_campbell_concepts_5/media/objects/3993/4089385/assets/labbench/learning/osmosis/concepts.html


 

Recently, the CDC has recognized that air transmission via aerosols is the primary infection method.  This has evolved prevention to center more on the wearing of masks rather than washing hands.  

 

  1. Science is a learning process and is not perfect.  How does misunderstanding this reality lead to poor decisions?

 

  1. Provide at least two examples of decisions people might make which do not support the common good.

 

Slide L

 

EXAMPLES FROM HISTORY (teacher may choose however many you prefer to address)

Let’s work through two examples. One due to a lack of scientific knowledge, and one due to a moral failure. 

 

  1. Spanish flu of 1918 - A lack of scientific knowledge prevented treatment. Virology didn’t exist yet.

 

  1. The AIDS epidemic in the 1980s - We consider this a moral failure. The stigma of AIDS initially prevented significant funding and investigation which delayed the development of an effective treatment.

https://www.niaid.nih.gov/diseases-conditions/hiv-vaccine-research-history

https://ftp.historyofvaccines.org/multilanguage/content/articles/development-hiv-vaccines

 

  1. Polio - This disease also suffered from a lack of scientific knowledge, but the development of a vaccine over approximately 60 years which was embraced with very little vaccine hesitancy.

https://www.historyofvaccines.org/timeline/polio

https://www.historyofvaccines.org/content/articles/cultural-perspectives-vaccination

https://vaxopedia.org/2018/04/04/the-catholic-church-and-vaccines/

 

https://www.npr.org/sections/health-shots/2021/05/03/988756973/cant-help-falling-in-love-with-a-vaccine-how-polio-campaign-beat-vaccine-hesitan 

 

Slide M

We have worked to understand the common good. We have seen how science and faith can work together to make ethical decisions. We have gone through examples of how science has tried to guide us through the recent pandemic - even though our early understanding of the science might not be perfect.  Now we are going to give you a chance to work in a group on one final scenario using what you have learned.


 

Slide N

 

Summative Assessment Scenario - students will work as a group to decide  “Who should be saved during a flu outbreak when there is not enough life-saving vaccine available for everybody?“ see the attached worksheets “Who Would You Save?” Students will present their choice of who to save and how they came to that decision.
