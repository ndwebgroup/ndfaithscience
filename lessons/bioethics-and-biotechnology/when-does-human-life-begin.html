When Does Human Life Begin?

  • Edit
  • Delete
(Rockhurst High School) This lesson is designed to be done in two parts. The first part of the lesson examines some of the different definitions of life ranging from the biological veiwpoint to the catholic viewpoint. The conclusion from both perspectives is the same: human life begins at conception. The second part of the lesson discusses how this conclusion may affect your understanding of abortion.

Essential Questions

1. Is the Catholic Church’s teaching on the beginning of life compatible with the scientific definition?

2. How does our understanding of the church and science’s definition of the beginning of life affect one’s worldview on issues of human dignity ie abortion?

3. If they are truly compatible, why is there such a divide in public opinion on abortion/assited suicide?

 

Knowledge

1. Students will KNOW different definitions of life.

 

2. Students will KNOW the 8 characteristics of life that most biologist use to define life.

 

3. Students will KNOW the key stages of fetal development.

 

4. Students will KNOW the key points of the intellectual and philosophical tradition upon which the Church defines life.


 

Skills

  1. Students will be able to explain how life begins from a scientific viewpoint.
  2. Students will be able to explain how life begins from a religious viewpoint.
  3. Students will analyze and evaluate if something is living or not based on their understanding of the characteristics of life.
  4. Students will be able to articulate (either in speech or writing) how these understandings of the beginning of life affect their understanding on abortion for reasons such as…
    1. Medical reasons
    2. Eugenics
    3. Rape
    4. Incest
    5. Convenience

Method

  1. Lesson Timing:
    1. one 80 min lesson with a 5 min break in between
      1. We chose this method because our school is on a block schedule
  2. Lesson plan structure
    1. The lesson will be broken up into two sections, with the first section answering the question: are the Church’s teaching on the beginning of life and science’s teaching compatible? Once we have established that they are, the second half of class will be spent answering the question: how should this compatibility inform our worldview? And why is there such a divide in public opinion on issues such as abortion and assisted suicide? Below are the steps:
      1. Introductory prayer
      2. Go over the objectives
      3. Have the class propose different definitions for life 
      4. Provide several dictionary definitions of life and a biological definition
      5. Ask the students, during the fetal development, at what point does life BEGIN? 
      6. Provide the timeline for fetal development (provide video and writeup)
      7. What are the 8 characteristics of life?
      8. Check-in/reflection:  Based on what we’ve covered so far, what does science have to say about the beginning of life?
      9. Have the class guess what the Church’s teaching on the beginning of life is.
      10. Provide the Church’s teaching
      11. Where does the Church get this rationale?
        1. https://www.usccb.org/beliefs-and-teachings/what-we-believe/catholic-social-teaching/life-and-dignity-of-the-human-person
        2. https://www.usccb.org/issues-and-action/human-life-and-dignity/abortion/respect-for-unborn-human-life
      12. Check in: Is the Catholic Church’s teaching on the beginning of life compatible with the scientific definition?
      13. BREAK
      14. If life has begun according to both science and faith, our legal system says you have a right to life.
        1. So how do we now address abortion?

Standards

The STANDARDS satisfied in this lesson are:

 

Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS):

 

Scientific Knowledge is Open to Revision in Light of New Evidence

  • Most scientific knowledge is quite durable, but is, in principle, subject to change based on new evidence and/or reinterpretation of existing evidence. (HS-LS2-3)
  • Scientific argumentation is a mode of logical discourse used to clarify the strength of relationships between ideas and evidence that may result in revision of an explanation. (HS-LS2-6),(HS-LS2-8)


 

Scientific Investigations Use a Variety of Methods

  • Scientific inquiry is characterized by a common set of values that include: logical thinking, precision, open-mindedness, objectivity, skepticism, replicability of results, and honest and ethical reporting of findings. (HS-LS1-3)

I.B.3 What it means to be a follower of Christ.

 

II.B.5.d Sins against the Fifth Commandment: murder; suicide; abortion; euthanasia;. . . abuse . . . of the body.

 

V.A.3 In reality, sinful actions diminish freedom; moral acts increase it.
