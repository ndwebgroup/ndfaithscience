The survey is meant to determine whether students perceive whether there is a conflict between faith and science. </strong></p> <ol> </ol> <p><strong>2.. Assign 7 groups of 3 or 4 to research an assigned task.  Students in each group will first be asked a series of leading questions about a Catholic scientist. After they have answered these questions in their group, they will then do research on the scientist they have identified. See attached file on Scientist group work</strong></p> <p dir="ltr">                                          </p> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Group 1</strong></p> <p dir="ltr"><strong>The ecosystem of North and South America is imperiled with a deadly virus. Scientists believe conditions on Ceres would best facilitate  experiments for a cure. These experiments are risky and might cause great harm to a team of twelve members that will seek a cure. The vaccine they develop might cause even greater harm if used on earth. Would you be willing to live on Ceres for six months to work on a cure?</strong></p> <ol> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Where and what is Ceres?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>How long will it take to arrive at Ceres?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Who discovered Ceres?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Why do you think this person was motivated to discover Ceres?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Do you think this discoverer ever envisioned the possibility that Ceres could be helpful to mankind? Why</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Would you include a combination of scientists and theologians on this team?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What criteria would you establish for the members of this team?</strong></p> </li> </ol> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Group 2</strong></p> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Two students walk out of Cardinal Hayes ( local school name )after a rain shower and see a bright rainbow over the Grand Concourse (change to local street). One student from Mrs. Obensco's Theology class says the rainbow is most significant because it is a symbol of the covenant God made with humankind.  He cites Genesis 9:13 and Sirach 43:12 to defend her position. Another student from Father Chuilog's Astronomy class says rainbows have nothing to do with God and cites some paragraphs from a principle that dates back to the early 1600s.  As they argue a third student offers some more questions to consider.</strong></p> <ol> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What process causes the rainbow to occur?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>How does this process occur?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Who was the first person to describe this process?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What do we mean by literal and spiritual interpretations of the Bible?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>How did ancient pagans understand rainbows?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What do we mean when we speak of primary and secondary causality?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Can the answers to questions 2, 4 and 6 allow the theology student and the astronomy student to find common ground?</strong></p> </li> </ol> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Group 3: </strong></p> <p dir="ltr"><strong>A mission to end starvation in Domerland is threatened by sunspots and solar storms. These storms are interfering with the GPS needed to guide the ships with food, water and medicine to this remote harbor in the Arctic Circle. Without proper guidance, the ships may crash, and lives may be lost. The chances of rescuing Domerland are very slim, yet there is one Christian organization willing to risk all to save lives. The only group of scientists who can lend immediate technical assistance believe the rescue mission is impossible. As professed social Darwinists they initially refuse to help, but when promised grant money for their other research projects they agree to collaborate.</strong></p> <ol> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What are sunspots ?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>How do sunspots and solar storms disrupt cell phone and GPS use?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Who was the first person to discover sunspots?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Why would someone study the sun and its surface?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Do you think this discoverer ever imagined his research might be used to save lives? Why?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What dilemmas do scientists face when they prioritize their research projects?</strong></p> </li> </ol> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Group 4:</strong></p> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Matt is passionate about basketball, and wants to have a son who is very tall.  He thinks that if he marries a tall woman, his son will certainly be tall also. What might you tell Matt about genetics and the inheritance of physical traits ?</strong></p> <ol> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What are “genes”?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Who is considered the father of genetics ?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What motivated this person to study how different traits are inherited from one generation to another?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Is Matt using “genetic engineering”?</strong></p> </li> </ol> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Group 5:</strong></p> <p dir="ltr"><strong>In 1925, Clarence Darrow defended a high school biology teacher in Tennessee for teaching something that was considered controversial.  The teacher was eventually found guilty, and for his penalty he had to pay a one dollar fine. About 300 years earlier, an Italian scientist defended himself for something he was teaching. However the Church found him guilty of “vehement suspicion of heresy”.   His penalty was that he was no longer to teach on this subject, and was placed under house arrest.</strong></p> <ol> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Name the biology teacher from Tennessee, and identify the topic that caused the controversy.</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Name the Italian scientist, and identify the topic that caused the controversy.</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Define what heresy is.</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>In what ways are these two incidents similar ?</strong></p> </li> </ol> <p><strong>Group 6:</strong></p> <p dir="ltr"><strong>The day after an exam students are discussing the question that appears below. Some feel the question shows a bias toward atheism or agnosticism. Other students do not feel the same way. How could a dialogue between these two sides bring more clarity?</strong></p> <p dir="ltr"><strong>The August 14, 2014 Earth Science New York State Regents Exam begins with the question:  "Which evidence best supports the theory that the universe was created by an explosion called the Big Bang?"</strong></p> <ol> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Impact craters found on earth</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Cosmic background radiation</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>The different compositions of terrestrial  and Jovian planets</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>The blue shift of light from distant galaxies</strong></p> </li> </ol> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Use the following questions to consider all the implications of the exam question:</strong></p> <ol> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What does the Big Bang theory say about the universe?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>When did the Big Bang occur?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Who created the Big Bang Theory?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Why was the theory created?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What is another theory of how the universe was created?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Does one of these theories deny the truth of the other? Explain.</strong></p> </li> </ol> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Group 7</strong></p> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Jackson is very interested in computer science, and wants to learn how to code.  He finds a group of students who are also interested in coding, and they are planning to ask the principal to start a club after school where students can learn to code.  They are even thinking of asking girls from local Catholic high schools to join the club. Since there is already a Robotics Club in the school, Mr. Kitson, the principal, does not wish to give permission for a coding class and asks Jackson to lead a Christian service club to assist struggling students at the local daycare center.   After a week passes Jackson requests to meet with Principal Kitson to further discuss his proposal.</strong></p> <ol> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What is “coding” and why is important for computers?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What are the ways "coding" improves the quality of daily life?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>What are two coding languages used today?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>One of the earliest computer languages was BASIC. What woman, the first to earn a Ph.D. in computer science, helped develop this language?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>How did this woman's project fit into the overall goal of the organization to which she belonged?</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>To what extent does Principal Kitson's suggestion for a Christian service club have merit? Are there any shortcomings to Principal Kitson's suggestion</strong></p> </li> </ol> <p dir="ltr"><strong>The students should identify the following persons:</strong></p> <ol> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong> Fr. Giuseppe Piazzi, C.R.</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Fr. Francesco Maria Grimaldi, S.J.</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Fr. Christopher Scheiner, S.J.</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Fr. Gregor Mendel. O.S.A</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>John Thomas Scopes, Galileo Galilei</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Fr. Georges LeMaitre, S.J.</strong></p> </li> <li dir="ltr"> <p dir="ltr"><strong>Sr. Mary Keller, B.V.M.</strong></p> </li> </ol> <p dir="ltr"><strong>3. Groups input data in google slides. </strong></p> <ul dir="ltr"> <li>Name of Scientist with dates.</li> <li>Picture of Scientist</li> <li>Famous Discovery in a few lines</li> <li>What proof can we find to prove they were people of faith?</li> <li>How might their faith have influenced their scientific studies?</li> </ul> <p dir="ltr"><strong>4.  Each group shares with the class about the scientist they have researched, Then the class is guided in a discussion about  the relationship/”conflict” between faith and science. Leading questions to spark class discussion could be 1) what surprised you the most as you did your research and heard the presentations about Catholic scientists 2)  What kind of skills make a good scientist, and could these same skills help someone develop a deeper faith ?  3) Why do you think so many people think that science and religion are in conflict, and what can we do about it ?  4) Do you think God encourages scientists to discover new things and explore new places ?  5)  Does having a deeper understanding of how the world works (through science) take away from believing that God made the world ?  6) How is "wonder" both important to science and religion ?  6)  Does science leave a place for God, or does it push HIm out ?  7) What kinds of questions does science ask, and what kinds of questions does faith ask?</strong></p> <p dir="ltr"><strong>5. Worksheet on Scientists and Scripture passages (see attached file) is distributed. Results discussed in class. </strong></p> <p dir="ltr"><strong>6. Lesson Extension: Production of a video  ( We will collaborate with our Media Department to develop a 3-4 minute video. A student will interview the seven scientists who will speak in the first person singular. Each scientist will identify himself, offer a scientific insight and offer a verse of Scripture that has inspired their research.</strong></p> <p dir="ltr"><strong> Video is shown school wide. <p>For a half or full class period, the instructor gives general background on the origin of the myth that faith and science are in conflict.  Depth is at the judgement of the instructor.  Included in resources is a simple way to introduce the students to scientists of faith using PowerPoint slides. The final point given to the students is that God is the origin of all knowledge and truth no matter how that knowledge was acquired by humanity.</p> <p>Part II:  Collection of information and creation of trading cards</p> <p>The amount of class time devoted to student research and development of the card is determined by the instructor.   Suggested time allotment is one day for research in class with the development of the card due a day or two later.  Development can also be done during a class period.  An example of a research page is included in the resources.  The trading card should be about the size of a 3 x 5 note card, however, the size of the note card should fit the students' abilities.  Display of the information on the card can vary, but a suggested layout is included in the resources.  The trading card may be created electronically, or hand drawn.</p> <p>Part III:  Sharing of knowledge</p> <p>The final part of the lesson is for the students to share and display the information gathered about their scientist of faith.  This can be accomplished by a variety of methods.   One suggestion is presentation of the card information/design to others in the class.   This should be limited to about a 5 minute overview of what the student has researched.  The trading card or larger posters can be displayed around the classroom or school building.  A  bingo style scavenger hunt allows the students to learn from each other's research and a blank bingo form is given in the resources.  Another suggestion for sharing information includes the <em>Cross the Line</em> activity.  Cross the Line is similar to an icebreaker activity but the students will pretend they are answering as the scientist they have researched. </p> <p>Ask everyone to gather on one side of the room/line and face towards its center.</p> <p>Instructions (you read these to your students) I will call out specific categories/labels/descriptions, and ask that all of those to whom this applies, walk to the other side of the room/line. For example, I might request that anyone who has a scientist with glasses please cross the room. If this describes your scientist you would walk to this side of the room/line. Once there, turn and face the crowd you just left.  After a few seconds,everyone will return to the same side and  I will continue with a new question.  At the conclusion of the activity, we will discuss how often the scientists had something in common.</p> <p>An alternative would be to do this in a circle with one volunteer in the middle. All the students would find new seats instead of crossing the line. So you could say if your scientist lived in Europe move your seat.
